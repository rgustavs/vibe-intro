Prompting
for PMs.

A short, practical guide to writing prompts that ship —
the way you'd write a spec, not a wish.

Audience Product Managers Length 12 slides Reading ~10 min
01 · Why this matters

The prompt is the spec now.

When a feature is powered by a language model, the prompt encodes the product behavior. It defines what the feature can do, how it sounds, what it refuses, and what it returns.

If you ship AI features and you can't read the prompt, you're shipping a feature you can't review.

Old world
Spec → Engineer → Behavior

Behavior was implemented in code. Reviewing the spec meant reviewing the product.

New world
Spec → Prompt → Behavior

Behavior lives in the prompt. The prompt is now part of the product surface area you own.

02 · Definition

A prompt is every word the model sees before it answers.

System messages, role instructions, conversation history, retrieved documents, the user's question — all of it is the prompt. The model has no other source of intent. It cannot read your roadmap, your Slack, or your mind.

# everything in this box is "the prompt" System: You are an assistant that... Context: [retrieved docs] User: Summarize last week's bugs.

Whatever isn't in the prompt, the model has to guess.

03 · Anatomy

Five parts of a useful prompt.

Role

Who the model is. "You are a research synthesizer for a B2B PM team."

Context

What it needs to know. Background docs, definitions, constraints.

Task

The single thing to do. One clear instruction, not five.

Examples

What good looks like. One or two worked cases.

Format

The shape of the output. Markdown, JSON, length, fields.

You don't need all five every time. You need the ones the model can't safely guess.

Principle 1

Specificity beats cleverness.

vague Summarize this user feedback.
specific Summarize these 12 user interviews into the 5 most frequent themes. For each theme: a one-line headline, two supporting quotes, and the count of interviews mentioning it.

The second version isn't longer because it's verbose. It's longer because it actually says what you want.

Principle 2

Give the model what you already know.

no context Write a launch email for our new bulk-export feature.
with context Write a launch email for our new bulk-export feature. Audience: existing customers on the Growth plan — mostly ops managers who export one row at a time today. Why we built it: 14 customer interviews this quarter mentioned manually copy-pasting rows out. Tone: practical, no superlatives. Length: under 150 words. CTA: one link to a 2-min demo.

Same task. The second one will sound like your team wrote it. The first one will sound like every other launch email on the internet.

Principle 3

Show what good looks like, then ask.

no example Write a release note for our new bulk-export feature. → generic marketing copy
one example Here's how we write release notes: Filters that stick Filters now persist across page reloads. Saves a step for anyone working through the queue. Now write one for: bulk export. → matches your voice immediately

One good example is worth more than three paragraphs of instructions on tone. Models are pattern matchers — give them a pattern.

Principle 4

Decide the shape of the answer before you ask.

For code
JSON

Strict schema, enumerated fields, no prose. Downstream code can parse it.

For humans
Markdown

Headers, short paragraphs, bullets. Capped length.

For decisions
Structured

Recommendation, confidence, reasoning, alternatives — in that order, every time.

Output format is the single cheapest lever to make AI features feel less random.

04 · Iteration

Prompts are products. Iterate them like products.

  • Version them. Diff them in PRs.
  • Build a small eval set of real inputs.
  • Test against the set, not against the one example that's top of mind.
  • When a prompt regresses, treat it like a bug.
  • When you ship a new model, re-run your evals.

A prompt that works once is a demo. A prompt that works on the eval set is a feature.

05 · Pitfalls

Where prompts quietly go wrong.

"Don't do X"

Negative instructions are unreliable. Tell the model what to do instead.

Buried instruction

The task arrives at the end of a long context dump. Put the ask near the top, repeat it near the bottom.

Cherry-picked testing

"It worked once." It needs to work on the boring, weird, and adversarial inputs too.

Conversational format

"Make it short and nice" is mood, not format. Specify length and structure literally.

Worked example · 01 of 04

Synthesizing user research.

Turn 18 interviews into 3 themes.

You finished a round of customer interviews. You have transcripts. You don't have time to re-read them all.

This prompt does the first pass — and shows its work, so you can spot-check it.

  • Role
  • Context
  • Task
  • Format
prompt RoleYou are a research synthesizer for a B2B SaaS product team. ContextWe interviewed 18 customers about our onboarding flow. Our goal: the top 3 friction points to fix this quarter. "Friction" = anything that delayed a user reaching their first successful workflow run. TaskRead the transcripts below. Cluster mentions of friction into themes. Surface the 3 most- mentioned, ranked by frequency. FormatFor each theme return: - Headline (≤10 words) - Count of interviews mentioning it - 2 verbatim quotes (with speaker ID) - 1 hypothesized root cause - 1 follow-up question for round 2 Return as markdown, H3 per theme. Transcripts[paste 18 transcripts]
Worked example · 02 of 04

Putting numbers on fuzzy things.

Estimate impact with stated assumptions.

You have 8 feature ideas and a prioritization meeting tomorrow. You don't need precision. You need defensible estimates.

This prompt forces the model to show its assumptions — so you can argue with them instead of the conclusion.

  • Role
  • Numbers
  • Confidence
  • Table out
prompt RoleYou are an analyst who converts qualitative signals into rough quantitative estimates with explicit assumptions. ContextWe're prioritizing 8 feature ideas. We have: - 320 user-feedback items, 6 months - 42,000 monthly active users - Pricing: Free, Pro $29, Biz $99 - Quarterly target: +$120k MRR TaskFor each feature, estimate: 1. % of MAU likely affected (low / mid / high) 2. Signal strength in feedback (count of related mentions) 3. Plausible monthly revenue impact 4. Confidence (0–100%) State every assumption explicitly. Flag any estimate below 60% confidence with ⚠. FormatMarkdown table, one row per feature: Feature | %MAU (L/M/H) | Signal | $/mo | Confidence | Key assumption. Inputs[feature list] [feedback corpus]
Worked example · 03 of 04

A project as a source of truth.

What to keep in context, not in your prompt.

If your tool supports persistent project files, fill it once. Every prompt then runs against your actual product, not a hypothetical one.

  • Vision docOne-page product intent
  • OKRsQuarterly outcomes & metrics
  • ICPWho we sell to, who we don't
  • GlossaryTerms, acronyms, naming
  • RoadmapIn-flight and next-up
  • Style guideVoice for customer copy
  • Decision logWhat we chose, why
  • ConstraintsLegal, compliance, tech
a question this enables TaskI'm scoping the "bulk export" feature. Using the project files: 1. Which OKR does this serve? Quote the relevant line. 2. Is the target audience inside or outside our ICP? 3. Are any constraints in the legal doc triggered? Be precise. 4. Has a similar feature been proposed and declined before? Check the decision log. 5. Draft a one-line feature description in our public style-guide voice. FormatShort markdown brief. Each answer cites the project file it pulled from, by name. → no context-dump needed in the prompt; the project carries it.
Worked example · 04 of 04

Drafting the weekly update.

Raw notes in. Executive update out.

You finished the week with a notebook of bullets, half-thoughts, and a few Slack copy-pastes. The update is due in 20 minutes.

This prompt knows your audience, your voice, and what they actually care about — so you don't rewrite it from scratch every Friday.

  • Audience
  • Voice
  • What matters
  • Format
prompt RoleYou are an experienced PM drafting a weekly update for the executive team. AudienceCEO, CTO, Head of Sales. They have ~90 seconds to read it. They care about: - Shipped vs. planned - Risks to the quarter - Customer signal worth knowing - What blocked us They do not care about: - Internal team dynamics - Tool changes - JIRA ticket counts VoiceFactual, calm, concise. No hype, no apologies, no adverbs. TaskTurn the raw notes below into this week's update. Format - Subject line (≤8 words) - "What shipped" — 3 bullets max, each with impact - "What's at risk" — 2 bullets max, each with mitigation - "What we learned" — 1 bullet - "What I need from you" — 0–1 bullet, only if real Raw notes[paste week's notes]
In closing

Four things to remember.

01
Prompts are product surface area — own them.
02
Be specific, give context, show examples, specify format.
03
Iterate against a real eval set, not a vibe.
04
When in doubt, the prompt isn't too long — it's too vague.
